The Research

Millions order daily.
Many don't trust the experience.

DoorDash dominates delivery, but user research and reviews reveal trust-eroding UX patterns that hurt retention and create friction at the moments that matter most.

Hidden Fee Shock

Users consistently report surprise at checkout. Service fees, delivery fees, and "small order" fees stack up without clear explanation, destroying trust at the critical conversion moment.

Source: Trustpilot Reviews, Trustindex 2026

Home Screen Overload

The home screen suffers from "featuritis" — too many promotions, carousels, banners, and categories competing for attention. Users can't decide what action to take, leading to decision paralysis.

Source: IXD@Pratt Design Critique, UX Case Studies

Generic Recommendations

"DoorDash doesn't seem to have the type of restaurants I like." Users report the app doesn't learn their preferences, serving the same promoted content regardless of past behavior.

Source: UX Collective Case Study, User Research

Unreliable ETAs

Delivery times shown at browse don't match reality. Dashers picking up multiple orders makes food cold, and batched deliveries extend ETAs without clear communication.

Source: Trustpilot, Reddit r/doordash

Inconsistent Information

Restaurant details, menu availability, and pricing don't stay consistent across the app. Users encounter different information on the browse page vs. the restaurant page vs. checkout.

Source: Medium UX Analysis, App Store Reviews

Reorder Friction

Users can't easily find the option to reorder from past orders. The most frequent use case — "I want what I had last time" — is buried behind multiple taps and menu layers.

Source: UX Collective, Daffodil Software Analysis

The Solution

An AI-powered transparency layer that replaces clutter with context — showing personalized picks, honest pricing breakdowns, and real-time delivery intelligence before you order.

Before & After

From cluttered to clear

Without Clarity
Fee shock at checkout — hidden service fees, delivery fees, and upcharges surprise users after they've built their order
Cluttered home screen with promotions, banners, and carousels competing for attention
Generic recommendations that don't learn from ordering history or time-of-day patterns
Opaque delivery estimates — no context about batching, distance, or traffic conditions
With Clarity
Upfront price transparency — total cost with breakdown shown before you open the restaurant
AI-curated home screen with 3 personalized picks based on your patterns and mood
LLM-powered search — ask "cheap Thai under 30 min" and get instant, relevant results
Delivery intelligence — see batching status, distance, and traffic before you order

Implementation

Roadmap

A 12-week internship plan that ships incrementally — prototype, test with real users, and iterate.

Phase 1

Transparent Pricing

Design upfront cost breakdowns shown at the restaurant card level. A/B test "total price shown first" vs. current "subtotal only" pattern on conversion rates.

Weeks 1–3
Phase 2

AI-Curated Home

Replace carousel clutter with a personalized "Picked for you" section powered by DoorDash's LLM personalization framework. Test with time-of-day and mood signals.

Weeks 4–6
Phase 3

Natural Language Search

Prototype an AI search experience: "cheap Thai under 30 min" or "something new like what I usually order." Leverage DoorDash's existing LLM infrastructure.

Weeks 7–9
Phase 4

Delivery Intelligence

Surface real-time delivery context — batching status, Dasher distance, traffic conditions — giving users honest expectations before ordering.

Weeks 10–12

About the Designer

Maria Dumitrescu

I'm a design student passionate about using AI to create more honest, human product experiences. DoorDash Clarity represents my belief that transparency isn't at odds with growth — when users trust your pricing and recommendations, they order more, not less.

This proposal is grounded in real user research from Trustpilot, UX case studies, DoorDash's own engineering blog, and their KDD 2025 personalization research. I'm excited about experimenting with AI tools like Cursor and Claude Code to move from concept to prototype quickly.
