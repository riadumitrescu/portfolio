Why This, Why Now

The hardest part of AI Teammates is just beginning

In March 2026, AI Teammates went GA. These are collaborative AI agents that live inside projects, get assigned tasks, receive feedback, and build institutional memory over time. The early numbers look great. But here's the thing: the teams using it now chose to experiment. The next wave of users didn't. They're skeptical, or busy, or just not sure what an "AI agent" even means. And that's not a technology problem. It's a design problem.

Whether those teams end up trusting AI Teammates or ignoring them will come down to the experience, not the capability. That's what makes this such an interesting design challenge.

What I Bring

What building Nuuko taught me about AI trust

Nuuko is an AI-powered journaling app I built that surfaces patterns in your own writing over time. The AI reads your most personal thoughts and reflects insights back. It's a very high-trust interaction, and building it taught me a lot about what makes people comfortable (or uncomfortable) with AI:

Trust comes from experiencing one small success

When users saw their first Nuuko "Wrapped" summary and thought "oh, that's actually me," they were hooked. No onboarding tutorial created that moment. The AI's output did.

Tone matters as much as accuracy

When I tested a more clinical, data-driven tone, users felt surveilled rather than supported. The same insight, different words, completely different emotional response.

Doing less builds more trust than doing more

Nuuko's first version had too many AI features visible at once. When I reduced the surface to just journaling and one insight per session, engagement went up. People trusted it more because it tried to do less.

Research Directions

Three questions I'd investigate

If I were starting this internship, these are the first things I'd want to understand. Each one gets at a different layer of how teams actually learn to work with AI.

01

"What does the first 10 minutes with an AI Teammate feel like?"

Where does trust break in the first interaction? Where do people hesitate? When does the AI feel like a tool versus a teammate? When do they decide "this is useful" or "this is not for me"?

Moderated usability sessions — 6-8 first-time users
Design lens from Nuuko

Journaling prompts needed to feel like invitations, not instructions. "Tell me about your day" felt clinical. "What's one small thing that made you feel something today?" felt human. The same principle applies to how an AI Teammate introduces itself.

02

"How do teams develop shared understanding of what AI can and can't do?"

Most AI tools are single-player. Asana's AI Teammates are shared. When one person configures an agent, the rest of the team encounters it without that context. Does the team develop a shared mental model? Who becomes the "AI translator"?

Diary study — 3-4 teams over 2 weeks
Why this is unique to Asana

This multiplayer dimension is what makes Asana's approach fundamentally different from every other AI tool. If the team doesn't develop shared understanding, you get inconsistent expectations, frustration, and eventually abandonment.

03

"What role does tone and personality play in whether teams keep using an AI agent?"

Do people want their AI Teammate to have personality, or does personality feel uncanny? Does warmth increase trust, or feel manipulative? Does acknowledging uncertainty help?

Comparative prototype test — 3 communication styles, 5 per variant
Design lens from Nuuko

In Nuuko, the AI's tone was the single biggest factor in whether users felt "supported" versus "watched." The same pattern recognition, wrapped in different language, created completely different emotional responses.

Design Explorations

Six ideas I'd want to explore

None of these are final solutions. They're starting points that the research would sharpen, redirect, or throw out entirely. But they show how I'm thinking about the problem.

Idea 01

The "First Task" Guided Experience

Right now, setting up an AI Teammate feels like filling out a form. There's no moment where the team experiences what the AI can do before committing.

Design a guided "first task" where the AI walks the team through a low-stakes, reversible task together. A ride-along before you hand over the keys. The team picks a real (but small) task, watches the AI's thinking step by step, gives feedback, and sees the AI incorporate it.

Design principle

People don't trust AI by reading documentation. They trust it by experiencing one small win. Nuuko doesn't explain pattern recognition during onboarding. It just lets you journal for a few days and then shows you your first insight. That moment does the convincing.

Idea 02

Layered Transparency

How much AI reasoning should be visible, and when? Too much detail is noisy. Too little is a black box. Neither builds trust.

A three-layer transparency model inspired by clearly., my data visualization tool:

Layer 1 — Summary: One sentence, always visible. "I drafted the brief based on your strategy doc."
Layer 2 — Reasoning: Expandable. Sources, decisions, uncertainties.
Layer 3 — Full Trace: On demand. Complete audit trail for admins.
From clearly.

Non-technical users don't distrust data — they distrust their ability to interpret it. Leading with "sentences before charts" closes the confidence gap.

Idea 03

Configurable Communication Style

A startup design team and an enterprise compliance team communicate in fundamentally different ways. A one-size-fits-all AI voice will feel wrong to most teams.

Let teams configure not just what an AI Teammate does, but how it communicates:

Concise vs. Detailed — "Brief updated." vs. "I updated the brief with three changes..."
Formal vs. Casual — "The brief is ready for review." vs. "Hey team, brief's done!"
Proactive vs. On-demand — Waits for tasks vs. flags risks and suggests actions.

Over time, the AI could learn from how the team talks and adapt on its own. But starting with explicit configuration gives people a sense of control, which is really important early on.

Idea 04

Mobile-First AI Collaboration

AI Teammates are desktop-heavy. But people check Asana on phones constantly — during commutes, between meetings, in quick moments.

Lightweight mobile surfaces for AI collaboration:

AI Digest — Daily summary card: "Sprint Coach flagged 2 blocked tasks while you were out."
Swipeable Review — Quick approve/comment gestures for AI output, like triaging email.
Distinct Notifications — AI updates should look visually different from human updates.
Warsaw team relevance

The Warsaw design hub owns the Mobile group. This could be a direct contribution to that team's roadmap and would help AI Teammates reach people beyond desktop power users.

Idea 05

AI Teammate "Intro" Moments

When someone on the team sets up an AI Teammate, the rest of the team just... encounters it. There's no introduction, no context for what this thing does or why it's there.

What if the AI Teammate introduced itself when it joins a project? Not in a cringey "Hi I'm your AI!" way, but something simple and useful: "Hey, I was set up by [person] to help with [thing]. Here's what I can do, here's what I can't, and here's how to give me feedback." Like a new team member posting in the channel on their first day. It gives everyone the same baseline understanding instead of leaving people to figure it out on their own.

Idea 06

Trust Signals in the UI

Right now there's no easy way to tell how confident the AI is about its work, or how much human input shaped the output. Everything looks the same whether the AI is 95% sure or guessing.

Small, ambient signals that communicate confidence and provenance. Things like: a subtle indicator showing how many sources the AI referenced, whether a human reviewed the output, or if the AI flagged something it wasn't sure about. Not heavy-handed warnings, just honest context. The goal is to let people calibrate their trust naturally, the way you do with a colleague. You trust someone more when they say "I'm not sure about this part" than when they present everything with the same confidence.

Landscape

Where Asana can differentiate

Asana has something no other platform has: the Work Graph. AI Teammates know about deadlines, dependencies, who's doing what, and how work connects across teams. But having that context isn't enough if people don't trust the AI to use it well.

Platform Approach Gap
Microsoft Copilot Embedded in Office, single-player, prompt-driven No multiplayer context. Team can't see what AI did for someone else.
Slack AI Summarizes channels and threads on demand Reactive only. No proactive collaboration or task ownership.
Notion AI Document-level assistant for writing and editing No workflow context. Doesn't know deadlines, dependencies, or team structure.
Monday AI Automations with AI-generated content Rule-based. No agent memory or learning from feedback.
Asana Multiplayer AI agents with full Work Graph context Opportunity: combine structural advantage with emotionally intelligent interaction design.
